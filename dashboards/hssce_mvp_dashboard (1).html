Cuba Strategic Stabilization Assessment — WCIS v5.2 / AICN Calibrated
Cuba faces a structural economic crisis driven primarily by energy infrastructure collapse, Venezuelan subsidy decline, and accumulated sanctions pressure. The humanitarian dimension — persistent food insecurity, pharmaceutical shortages, and record outmigration — is now materially affecting US domestic policy costs in Florida and at the southern border. The signal base converges on MODERATE confidence that conditions for calibrated engagement exist but that structural blocking dynamics — primarily Chinese credit dependency and Cuban government opacity — prevent HIGH confidence in any normalization pathway.
KEY FINDINGS
1.
The primary driver of Cuban instability is infrastructure failure, not political resistance — a distinction that opens humanitarian-framed engagement lanes not available in purely political frameworks.
2.
Record outmigration represents a measurable US domestic policy cost of the status quo that is largely absent from the current policy cost-benefit calculus.
3.
Executive branch retains authority for phased administrative action without congressional involvement — sanctions expansion of licensed categories does not require legislative change.
4.
Chinese and Russian structural interests in maintaining Cuban adversarial utility create a significant blocking condition that any normalization pathway must account for.
5.
GAESA capture risk means tourism and investment normalization without governance benchmarks would primarily benefit military-affiliated commercial entities, not the Cuban population.
